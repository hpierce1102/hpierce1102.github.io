Self Expression

CASSANDRA DeWITT & LIA FANNING

We express ourselves in a variety of ways including: our clothing, dyed unnatural hair color, tattoos, piercings, and any other distinguishable accessories we decide to decorate ourselves with. Since we were young, we were always told to be unique and express ourselves in our own way. Should this very authentic type of self-expression be prevalent in the professional work environment or should it be covered up and left at home? Some may argue for the right to advocate self-expression in the workplace while others would like to stick to the traditional, more professional approach. While neither way is wrong, it is a good idea to see and evaluate both arguments for this particular case.

Historically, every generation has gone through some drastic measures to disassociate themselves with previous trends, when it comes to what is in your closet. In some ways, they try to push the boundaries of the previous decades. In 1910, women were expected to wear long skirts and have minimal skin showing, but in the 1920s women shortened their skirts, chopped off their hair, wore make up, and smoked cigarettes (Lee). This is seen by many as one of the biggest female rebellious movements in history. As the decades went on, the skirts got shorter and the trends evolved. Companies seem to think what you wear, in some way, defines how well you perform at work.

Body art is becoming more common among individuals in this day

and age. Tattoos and piercings have become a social norm. Looking at statistics, one out of ten Americans have a tattoo, while one out of three of them are between the ages of 25 to 30. Could there be a day when it is out of the norm to not be inked? A number of different companies have actually been encouraging this type of self-expression through their current and potential employees. Companies that encourage self-expression concerning tattoos include: Ford Motor Company, Barnes and Noble, and Wal-Mart. Tattoos that are considered offensive should be covered up during working hours either by clothing, makeup or band-aids. Although many companies are giving the okay for employees to show off their artistic selves with ink, some have a stricter approach. When it comes to tattoos and piercings, much like anything else, it is best to be conservative and appropriate in all interviews and work-related situations.

Although society is becoming more accepting of these artistic pieces, they still might draw negative attention to yourself, rather than fully seeing the skill set you have to offer. Self-expression needs to be seen as a sign of individuality, not rebellion. Dressing how you want shouldnt be a constant struggle of whether or not you will be accepted, but it should be an expression of your individual personalities.
